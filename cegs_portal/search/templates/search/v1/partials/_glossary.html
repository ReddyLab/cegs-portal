CCGR Glossary

Duke Center for Combinatorial Gene Regulation (CCGR): The ambitious goal of the Duke Center for Combinatorial Gene Regulation, a Center of Excellence in Genomic Science, is to make combinatorial studies of the function of regulatory elements and noncoding variants routine.

CEGS: The Centers of Excellence in Genomic Science (CEGS) program supports multi-investigator, interdisciplinary research teams working together to address biomedical problems through development of integrated, transformative genomic approaches.

Regulatory Elements

DNase I Hypersensitive Site (DHS): A region of chromatin in the genome that is more sensitive to cleavage by the DNase I enzyme. These regions are characterized by increased DNA accessibility, which is due to the local remodeling of the chromatin structure.[29]

Guide RNA (gRNA): a short RNA sequence that guides the editing of RNA transcripts or DNA.[30]

Chromatin Accessible Region (CAR): genomic regions where chromatin is open or loosened, allowing DNA-binding proteins and transcription factors to bind to their target sequences.[31]

Called Regulatory Element (CRE): Regulatory element found but label/name not assigned.

cCREs

Candidate Cis-regulatory Element (cCRE): regions of non-coding DNA which regulate the transcription of neighboring genes.[27]

A promoter-like sequence (PLS): A PLS is a candidate cis-regulatory element(cCRE) that's putatively labeled as a promoter.[27]

Proximal enhancer-like (pELS): is a candidate cis-regulatory element (cCRE) that is classified as an enhancer and is located within 2 kilobases (kb) of an annotated GENCODE transcription start site (TSS).[27]

Distal enhancer-like (dELS): elements are a type of candidate cis-regulatory element (cCRE) that are classified as putative enhancers.[27]

H3K4me3: An epigenetic modification to one of the histones that makes up the non-DNA portion of a chromatin structure.[28]

Distal enhancer-like (dELS): elements are a type of candidate cis-regulatory element (cCRE) that are classified as putative enhancers.[27]

Techniques and Screens

ATAC-STARR-seq: A massively parallel reporter assay (MPRA) that quantifies regulatory activity within accessible chromatin. It combines ATAC-seq (assay for transposase-accessible chromatin using sequencing) with STARR-seq (self-transcribing active regulatory region sequencing) to selectively assay the regulatory potential of accessible DNA.[1]

Flow-FISH CRISPR screen: A method combining Hybridization Chain Reaction Fluorescent In-Situ Hybridization (HCR-FlowFISH) with Flow Cytometry to quantify native transcripts within their endogenous loci following CRISPR perturbations of regulatory elements.[2]

Perturb-Seq: A high-throughput method that combines CRISPR-mediated gene inactivations with single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) to assess comprehensive gene expression phenotypes for each perturbation.[3]

Proliferation screen: A type of assay used to measure the rapid increase in cell numbers, often used to assess cell growth, division, and the effects of various treatments on cell proliferation.[4]

scCERES: single-cell (CERES) a computational method to estimate gene dependency levels from CRISPR-Cas9 essentiality screens while accounting for the copy-number-specific effect.[5]

wgCERES: whole-genome CERES (wgCERES) a computational method to estimate gene dependency levels from CRISPR-Cas9 essentiality screens while accounting for the copy-number-specific effect.[5]

Cell Lines

iPSC (Induced Pluripotent Stem Cells): Pluripotent stem cells generated directly from a somatic cell, capable of differentiating into any cell type in the body.[6]

K562: A human immortalized myelogenous leukemia cell line, derived from a 53-year-old female chronic myelogenous leukemia patient.[7]

NPC (Neural Progenitor Cells): Cells that can differentiate into various types of neural cells, including neurons and glial cells.[8]

CD8: A transmembrane glycoprotein that serves as a co-receptor for the T-cell receptor (TCR), predominantly expressed on cytotoxic T cells.[9]

MCF-7: A breast cancer cell line isolated from a 69-year-old woman, used extensively in breast cancer research.[10]

HCT116: A human colon cancer cell line used in therapeutic research and drug screenings.[11]

DU 145: A human prostate cancer cell line used in cancer research.[12]

A549: A human lung carcinoma cell line used in cancer research and drug testing.[13]

NCI-H460: A human large cell lung cancer cell line used in cancer research.[14]

PC-3: A human prostate cancer cell line used in cancer research.[15]

MDA-MB-231: A human breast cancer cell line used in cancer research.[16]

HepG2: A human liver cancer cell line used in cancer research.[17]

SW620: A human colorectal adenocarcinoma cell line used in cancer research.[18]

Functional Characterization Modalities

scCRISPRi: Single-cell CRISPR interference, a technique used to study gene function at single-cell resolution by inhibiting gene expression.[19]

CRISPRi: CRISPR interference, a technique that uses a catalytically dead Cas9 (dCas9) to inhibit gene expression.[19]

CRISPRa: CRISPR activation, a technique that uses dCas9 fused to transcriptional activators to increase gene expression.[20]

Reporter Assay: An assay that uses a reporter gene to measure the activity of a particular gene or regulatory element.[21]

Genome Assembly

hg-19: GRCh37 (hg19) an earlier human reference genome version. This version has been widely used in genomics projects, including long-term, global studies like the 1,000 Genomes Project.[22]

hg-38: genome assembly GRCh38 (hg38) from Genome Reference Consortium. Corrected and improved version of hg19.[22]

Genomic Terms

Gene: A gene is the basic physical and functional unit of heredity. Genes are made up of DNA and each chromosome contains many genes.[23]

Transcript: A messenger RNA (mRNA) molecule that carries genetic information from DNA to the cytoplasm, where proteins are made.[24]

Exon: The sections of DNA (or RNA) that code for proteins.[25]

Identifiers

Ensembl ID: is an identifier used by the Ensembl database to label features like genes, transcripts, exons, or proteins.[26]
